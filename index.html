About this result

These calculations are based on the noise prediction methods contained in Annex F of BS 5228-1:2009. The calculations assume that the intervening ground is hard and uses the simple barrier attenuation method.

Plant Noise Level

The unit for this is dB. Obtain the noise level by direct measurement of similar plant in the same mode of operation, or use the values given in Annexes C and D of BS 5228-1:2009. The actual noise measurement is LAeq,T. This is just a technical definition that means the average noise level during the measurement period T.

SWL or SPL

It is important to identify whether the noise level is sound pressure level (LP) or sound power level (LW). If the noise level is a sound pressure level then it is necessary to specify at what distance this was measured at.

Reference Distance

This field only needs to be specified when the plant noise level is expressed as a sound pressure level. The reference distance is the distance at which the noise level was measured. This often gets overlooked. Imagine if you were to take a measurement of some construction plant 1 m away - it could be quite loud and you could measure something like 80 dB (as an example). Now if you were to walk 100 m away, you would measure 40 dB (provided that there was nothing else interfering with your measurements). So, if you were to specify 80 dB or 40 dB as a sound pressure level measurement, they could be the same noise level or vastly different if you haven't also specified the distance you were away when you took the measurement.

% On Time

Normally noise from construction is assessed as an average noise level over a period of time. For instance, if you were operating between 8 am and 6 pm, you would actually be interested in a 10-hour average and this is expressed as LAeq,10hr. The units are in dB, but you may also include the word LAeq,10hr after the dB. For instance, you might write "the noise from the construction site is 73 dB LAeq,10hr at the nearest residential property." It is important to understand this because you have to consider for what duration each of your pieces of construction plant is operating as this has a bearing on the daily average. For example, your normal working day is from 8 am to 6 pm and you know you need to use an excavator from 8 am until 11 am (3 hours in total), you would make the % on time as 30% because 30% of a 10-hour working day is 3 hours. The formula for calculating the amound you can reduce the noise level is

10 x log10(%-On-Time/100)

Barrier Correction

BS 5228 has two methods of calculating how much barriers reduce noise: a complicated way and a simple way. This application uses the simple way. Basically, the reduction is noise due to the barrier is either 0 dB, -5 dB or - 10 dB. If the barrer does not obstruct line of sight between the piece of machinery and the receptor of the noise then you correct the noise level by 0 dB. If the top of the plant is just visible over the noise barrier the correct the noise level by 5 dB. When the noise barrier completely hides the noise source then correct the noise level by 10 dB. It is important to remember that noise barriers are solid and have no gaps in them. There are other assumptions about noise barriers that we will not go into. But, topographical features such as earth bunds can be treated the same way as noise barriers.

Distance from Source to Receptor

Now this one is a little more complicated. In fact BS 5228 has some quite complicated formulas for distance correction when there is "soft ground" between the source and the receptor. This application assumes that all the intervening ground is "hard". There are two formulas that are used to calculate the reduction in noise as you get further way from the source. The following formula is used where the plant noise level is defined as a sound power level:

20 x log10(Distance) + 8

When the plant noise level is expressed as a sound power level the below formula is used:

20 x log10(Distance from Source to Receptor / Reference Distance)

Façade Correction

Façade correction are not included in this application. But, it is fairly straightforward to incorporate them yourself. Reflections are accounted for by adding 3 dB to the results. There are two places that the noise is affected by reflections (at least according to BS 5228!): at the source or at the receptor. If the piece of plant is withing 1 or 2 m of a hard reflecting surface (not the floor which is already encorporated in the calculations) then add 3 dB to the Plant Noise Level If the receptor is at a facade (which it often is as the receptor is normally a building) then you should add 3 dB to the Total Sound Pressure Level.
